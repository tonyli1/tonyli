Building Community in Livestream Gaming

As part of a graduate course in User Centered Design, in Autumn 2021, our team of 4 from the University of Washington's HCDE Masters program had 8 weeks to design a solution that fosters and promotes community for online livestreaming audiences.

Overview


Design Problem

How can virtual communication in livestreaming platforms be improved with different techniques to foster better community between game viewers?

Motivation

With the COVID-19 pandemic limiting the ability for people to meet and connect with each other in person, gamers resorted to watching livestream videos of video games to stay connected with one another. Our team wanted to explore how to improve the experience of creating community for gamers who watch livestreams.

Final Presentation

Hypothesis

Gaming livestreams are a valuable way to find others with similar interests, but toxicity and an overwhelming number of messages are some factors that are holding the livestreaming platforms back from building stronger communities.

Thus, we hypothesized a platform that allows user to engage with other viewers of similar interests while filtering spam and toxic messages would improve community-building between livestream viewers.

Methods

Using an Agile approach, we designed a Figma prototype to allow users to block certain users, invite friends to private and public group chats within a stream, and filter patterns of unwanted language from chats.

Top methods used: interviews, surveys, design ethnographies, design studio, wireframes, and prototypes.

Research

Survey

We launched a survey on Reddit and on UW's Slack to gather preliminary data about livestream viewers. We asked about usage of livestreaming platforms, content watched, and interactions in livestreams to learn about current interactions and what areas to focus on.

From the 22 responses we received, most respondents watched livestreams on Twitch, with Gaming and eSports being the top two categories watched. Most watched on their mobile phone, by themselves. Most reported having interacted with other viewers on the streams, but a group of respondents mentioned not chatting at all.


Interviewing

We interviewed six livestream viewers to gather more information on user expectations for a stream, user moods and feelings with different interactions, and walkthroughs of the livestream experience in general, to provide insight on challenges.


Design Ethnography

We also observed user interactions during an eSports livestream watch party on Twitch, to observe and feel how gamers watch livestream events and interact with each other. We observed different streams ranging from tens to tens of thousands of concurrent viewers.

Defining the Problem and Solution


Personas and Problems

From our research, we identified 3 main personas, each with their unique issues. This was especially eye-opening for me, as it helped me better understand the diversity of needs and interests of the target audience, even from our relatively small sample sizes.

Casual Kyle: The Casual Viewer

Casual Kyle has trouble tracking chats and doesn't appreciate offensive or explicit language. Would benefit from filtering/muting chat messages based on message type or user profile.

Hardcore Henry: The Extreme Viewer

Hardcore Henry is focused on conversations about gameplay and skill. Would benefit from focused chat subchannels.

Lurking Lily: The Lurker

Lurking Lily doesn't want to engage with strangers but wants to be in the loop. Would benefit from a private chat space with known friends.

Storyboards and User Flow Diagrams

Our team created a set of storyboards and user flows to understand how our personas would use our design solution. I found this much easier with our personas defined - my first taste of the power of personas!

Visual Design


Design Studio, Sketches, and Wireframes

Our team conducted 5-minute design studio exercised to sketch potential design layouts based on our understandings of the problem from our personal research and from competitor research. I had created wireframes before, but usually not so many so quickly! This was important to get the bare essentials recorded.

From our design studio, we identified the key themes and functions to incorporate into our design, creating wireframes from those sketches and flows to show how users would interact with our system. Also my first time using Figma for wireframing/prototyping...


Mockup/Prototype

We then created mockups and a prototype for usability testing. We aimed to keep consistency with user expectations for a livestreaming platform and a user-friendly design without needing to teach users how to use the app.

Usability Testing and Iteration


Usability Testing

Our team created a usability test plan that outlined purpose, user tasks, and our approach. This allowed us to test users and provide design insights to update the prototype.

Design Iteration

Fewer and Simpler Icons

Reduce affordances with clicking icons by differentiating, combining, or disambiguating placements. Almost all participants were confused about the social icon.

Easy and Quick Muting

Hovering is faster than clicking, with lower barrier to discover and perform.

Clear/Deliberate Interactions If Requires Greater Commitment

Users were notably distressed by immediate payment options after exploring donate feature.

Flexible Views

Personalize the viewing experience for different needs/goals.
